Evaluation of Whether Prenatal Metals Exposures and Birth Outcomes
are Mediated by Epigenomic and/or Metabolic Profiles

Environmental exposures (e.g., heavy metals) may perturb epigenetic programming and metabolic homeostasis during key developmental periods including early gestation, thereby setting the stage for disease development later in life. High-throughput ‘omics’ technologies, such as epigenomics and metabolomics, offer unparalleled opportunities to examine physiologic processes associated with in utero exposures that may exert a lasting influence on lifelong health. Our goal is to examine relationships between exposures to heavy metals early in pregnancy and the epigenome, metabolome, and lipidome of the offspring at birth. Through this work, we will gain insight into specific pathways with disrupted epigenetic gene regulation and/or metabolic homeostasis in heavy-metal exposed newborns, and their potential role in latent disease development.

  • Aim 1: Examine associations between biomarkers of prenatal metals exposure and metabolites and lipid profiles in umbilical cord blood plasma (n=48). Metals will be analyzed via ICP-MS in maternal urine from the first trimester of pregnancy. Untargeted metabolomics via reverse phase MS profiling and shotgun lipidomics approaches will be used to profile the metabolome and lipidome.
  • Aim 2: Identify CpG sites differentially methylated by prenatal metals exposure in infant umbilical cord blood leukocytes (N=48) using the Infinium MethylationEPIC BeadChip (Illumina) platform (>850,000 CpG sites).
  • Aim 3: Explore evidence for lipid, metabolite, and epigenomic profiles as mediators of relationships between prenatal metal exposures and birth outcomes (e.g., birth weight).
