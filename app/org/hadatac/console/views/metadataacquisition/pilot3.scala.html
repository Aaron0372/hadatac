Metal Mixtures and Birthweight and Biomarkers of Cumulative Oxidative Stress

Biological hypersensitivity to environmental stimuli is a fundamental feature of atopy, predisposing individuals to a spectrum of disorders, allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis, and allergic asthma. Evidence linking psychological stress to expression of atopy has grown with our increased understanding of the natural history and pathophysiology of atopic disorders and the neurobiology of stress vulnerability. However, the specific pathways that increase vulnerability to developing atopy in response to stress remain to be elucidated in human studies. Findings suggest that stressors influence pathogenesis by causing dysregulated biobehavioral states (e.g., depression, posttraumatic stress disorder), which in turn exert effects on physiological processes that influence disease risk. Traumatic events in particular, by definition threatening and distressful, are especially likely to lead to persistent psychological and physiological alterations. An important step toward identifying children at risk for costly pediatric disorders such as allergic asthma is the identification of potential mechanisms that may lead to and maintain early predisposition. Such evidence will inform more efficacious prevention and intervention strategies.

  • Aim 1: Examine associations between increased prenatal metal mixtures (Pb, Cd, As, Hg) and lower birthweight for gestational age.
  • Aim 2: Examine associations between increased prenatal metal mixtures (Pb, Cd, As, Hg) and a cumulative index of mitochondrial dysfunction based on long-range PCR in placental DNA.
  • Aim 3: Examine associations between metal mixtures and signature changes in metabolite expression in plasma. These metabolite expression changes will mediate the relationship between prenatal metal mixtures and smaller birthweight for gestational age.

Metals are important biologically, with complex functions. Many are both nutrients and toxicants. Mixtures of metals are a real life exposure scenario. There are no published studies of metal mixtures and fetal growth nor intermediate biomarkers that may inform underlying mechanisms. Understanding these relationships will be important to implement prevention programs.
