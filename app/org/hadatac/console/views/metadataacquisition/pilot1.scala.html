Urine Biomarkers for Eosinophilic Esophagitis

Eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE), a rapidly emerging allergic disease affecting all ages, presents with esophageal food impaction, dysphagia and esophageal stricturing. EoE is currently diagnosed and assessed with endoscopic biopsies that show major increases in eosinophil number and inflammation in only the esophagus. Generally, EoE is related to a food allergy and resolves if the inciting food is removed from the diet. However, IgE antibody tests generally do not identify the causative food and repeat endoscopic biopsies are required to assess disease activity. Endoscopy is expensive, requires sedation or anesthesia, and risks bleeding or perforation. Repeated endoscopy to assess treatment is not appealing to patients with EoE. A non-invasive method to assess disease activity in patients with established EoE is needed and we believe that a urine or serum test for assessing disease activity in EoE patients will be useful clinically and also will help increase our understanding of the biology of this important condition.

  • Aim 1: Metal search: metal toxicity can be manifest by inflammation. Hence, measuring metal levels may prove informative about the possible etiology of EoE, which is currently unknown. We plan to compare metal levels in the urine samples we will submit.
  • Aim 2: Inflammation marker search: Since inflammation is a hallmark of EoE, we will measure prostaglandin E2 metabolite (PGE-M), a marker of inflammation. We hypothesis that PGE-M will be elevated in the urine of subjects with active EoE.
  • Aim 3: Based on our prior metabolomic studies there are metabolites that could provide informative data regarding disease activity in patients with EoE. The subjects' samples submitted for this CHEAR proposal will be a more focused group of samples from individuals with active EoE and from these same individuals with inactive EoE.

The long-term goal of this research is to improve non-invasive methods to assess disease activity in EoE patients and increase our knowledge of EoE biology, with the objective of finding urine and serum metabolites associated with EoE disease activity. The central hypothesis of this research is that the major inflammatory process and the large number of eosinophils sloughed into the intestinal tract of EoE patients will result in altered serum and urine metabolite levels. This hypothesis is supported by preliminary analyses of samples from our urine repository, demonstrating impressive changes of urinary metabolites in EoE patients compared to controls. Identification of EoE metabolite biomarkers would allow easy, inexpensive, non-invasive assessment of EoE disease activity, obviate the need for endoscopy and allow easy follow-up of EoE patients.
